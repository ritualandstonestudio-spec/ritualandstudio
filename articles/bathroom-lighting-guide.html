Bathroom Lighting Is an Afterthought for Most Renovators. Here's Why It Shouldn't Be.

December 2025  ·  5 min read

Think about the last bathroom that genuinely stopped you. The one in a hotel, a restaurant, a friend's house that you could not quite stop thinking about. Chances are, you attributed it to the tiles, or the stone, or the tapware. But pull those images apart and you will almost always find that what you were really responding to was the light.

Lighting is the finish that makes or breaks every material decision you have already made. Get it wrong and the most beautiful bathroom in the world feels like a changing room. Get it right and even modest materials look extraordinary.

Why renovators leave it too late

Lighting gets decided last because electricians get called last. By the time most renovators are thinking about their light fittings, the budget has been spent, the decisions are exhausted, and the builder needs an answer by the end of the week. Whatever is fastest and easiest goes in.

The result is a bathroom lit by a single ceiling downlight positioned directly over the vanity, casting unflattering shadows directly downward on the face. It is the lighting equivalent of institutional fluorescence. The tiles are beautiful. The tapware is perfect. The room still feels wrong, and no one can quite say why.

The three layers of bathroom lighting

Ambient is your base layer: the general illumination that makes the room functional. This is typically ceiling-mounted, and it is what most renovators stop at. It is necessary but not sufficient.

Task is the light that does specific work. At the vanity mirror, this means lighting that falls on the face rather than the top of the head. A well-placed vertical strip either side of the mirror, or a horizontal fitting above and slightly in front of the mirror plane, eliminates the shadow problem entirely. In a shower recess, a waterproof fitting on the side wall rather than the ceiling creates a completely different quality of light.

Accent is the layer that elevates a bathroom from functional to atmospheric. A small LED strip inside a niche. A recessed fitting that grazes along a stone wall, picking up the texture. A single pendant above a freestanding bath. These are the details that read in photographs and that you feel without being able to name when you are actually in the room.

Colour temperature is not optional

The single easiest lighting mistake to fix, and the one most often made, is choosing the wrong colour temperature. Cool white (4000K and above) is clinical and unflattering. It is fine for a laundry. It is a disaster for a bathroom where you start and end your day.

Warm white, in the 2700K to 3000K range, is what you see in every spa, every luxury hotel, every bathroom that made you linger. It is warmer on skin tones, softer on the room, and far more aligned with how you want to feel in that space first thing in the morning.

Every fitting in the bathroom should be the same colour temperature. Mixing warm and cool sources in a single room is immediately noticeable, even if you cannot identify why the room feels unsettled.

Plan for dimmers from the start

A bathroom that cannot be dimmed is a bathroom that cannot shift modes. The same room that needs to function for a sharp morning routine should be able to soften completely for an evening bath. This is only possible if the electrical rough-in includes dimmer-compatible circuits. It costs almost nothing to specify at framing stage. It is expensive and disruptive to add after the walls are closed.

Lighting is not the glamorous part of a renovation. It does not have the tactile satisfaction of a tile selection or the visual drama of a tapware upgrade. But it is the one decision that determines whether all of those other decisions land the way you imagined they would. It deserves to be planned first, not last.
