Travertine, Marble, or Porcelain? A Practical Guide to Luxury Stone for Australian Homes

February 2026  ·  6 min read

Stone is the material that separates a bathroom that looks luxury from one that feels it. The weight, the temperature, the variation in surface and tone — these are qualities that no synthetic material has yet replicated with any real conviction. But stone is not all the same. Each type has a distinct personality, a maintenance requirement, and a set of conditions under which it excels.

This is the guide we wish every client had read before they walked into their first tile showroom.

Travertine

Travertine is a sedimentary limestone formed around natural springs, which gives it its characteristic pitting and variation. It is one of the warmest, most tactile stones available. In a bathroom, particularly one with warm lighting and natural materials, travertine creates an atmosphere that feels genuinely resort-like without trying very hard.

It comes in cream, ivory, walnut, and silver tones. The filled and honed finish is the most practical for bathrooms — it seals the natural voids and creates a surface that is cleanable and durable. The unfilled version, with its natural pitting, is beautiful but impractical for wet areas.

Best for: Warm, tactile bathrooms. Pairs beautifully with timber vanities, brushed brass tapware, and warm lighting. Requires sealing annually in wet areas.

Marble

Marble is the most universally recognised luxury stone and for good reason. The veining, the depth, the sheer variety of quarry sources around the world give it a visual richness that no other material matches. Calacatta, Carrara, Statuario, Nero Marquina — each has a distinct character and works in very different contexts.

The honest caveat with marble in a bathroom is maintenance. Marble is porous and reactive to acids, including many common cleaning products. In a bathroom used daily, it will etch, stain, and wear over time unless it is sealed regularly and cleaned with pH-neutral products. This is not a reason to avoid it. It is a reason to go in clear-eyed.

A marble bathroom that is maintained well looks extraordinary after twenty years. One that is not will show its age. The question is whether that maintenance commitment is realistic for your household.

Best for: Considered, design-led bathrooms where the stone is the hero. Requires sealing every 6 to 12 months and careful product selection for cleaning.

Porcelain stone-look

Porcelain that mimics natural stone has improved dramatically over the past decade. The best examples from Italian and Spanish manufacturers are genuinely difficult to distinguish from the real thing at first glance. They offer the visual quality of stone with a fraction of the maintenance.

Porcelain does not need sealing. It is non-porous, resistant to staining, and largely impervious to the acids and cleaning products that damage natural stone. For a family bathroom, or for a client who wants the look of stone without the commitment, high-quality porcelain is not a compromise. It is a considered choice.

Where it falls short is in sensory quality. The weight underfoot, the natural temperature variation, the slight imperfection of a real stone surface — these are absent. For some clients, this does not matter. For others, it is the entire point.

Best for: High-traffic bathrooms, family homes, or clients who want the look of stone with minimal maintenance. Quality varies enormously between brands — specification matters.

There is no universally correct choice between these three materials. The right choice is the one that works with your palette, suits your maintenance tolerance, and feels right for the way you live in your home. Getting that match right is exactly what we are here to help with.
